01 — Context

Exploring Retail Performance Through Data

This project analyzes four years of retail sales data from a global superstore using Microsoft Excel. The dataset contains 9,800 transactions across customers, products, shipping methods, and geographic regions.

The goal was to answer a series of business questions through pivot table analysis while following additional questions that emerged throughout the investigation.

Business Questions

  • — Which state has the highest sales?
  • — Which products lead in order count and revenue?
  • — Which customer segment generates the greatest business value?
  • — Are there recurring seasonal patterns and year-on-year growth?
  • — Does shipping mode affect delivery time?
  • — How do product categories compare by order volume and revenue?
Dataset

9,800 Retail Transactions

Four years of retail sales data (2015–2018) covering customers, products, shipping methods, and regional performance across the United States.

Approach

Question-Driven Analysis

Each business question was answered using Excel pivot tables before drilling deeper into customer segments, seasonal trends, shipping performance, and product value as new insights emerged.

02 — Approach

Start With a Question. Follow the Answer.

Rather than building a comprehensive dashboard upfront, I started with 3 questions and let each finding generate the next one a more honest reflection of how real analysis actually works.

Step 01

Identify the Columns

Before touching any formula, I mapped each question to the columns it needed. A small step that prevents a common mistake using Order ID as a proxy for sales when the Sales column exists.

Step 02

Build the Pivot Tables

One pivot per question. State × Sales. Product × Quantity. Product × Revenue. Segment × Orders + Sales. Each sorted to surface the answer immediately without scanning rows.

Step 03

Follow the Curiosity

California kept appearing as the top state. The segment with the fewest orders turned out to spend the most per order. Every finding raised a new question and I followed each one.

Step 04

Drill Into Home Office

Once Home Office stood out, I broke it down further by state, by product, and by shipping preference to understand not just that it was valuable, but where and how to act on it.

03 — Process
01 / State Sales Analysis

California vs the rest of the country

Pivot table: State × Sum of Sales, sorted descending. California came in at $446,306, North Dakota at $919. A 485x difference. The Corporate segment alone in California ($144,731) outpaced North Dakota's entire sales figure, confirming that business presence, not just population, drives the gap.

02 / Product Analysis — Two Ways

Same question, two completely different answers

"Which item is the highest selling?" turned out to be two separate questions. By quantity: Staple Envelope with 47 orders and $1,675 in total sales. By revenue: Canon imageCLASS 2200 Advanced Copier at $61,599 from just 5 orders. Recognizing that ambiguity early shaped how I framed every subsequent question.

03 / Segment Analysis

The smallest segment spends the most

Consumer: 5,101 orders, $1,148,060 total. Home Office: 1,746 orders, $424,982 total. On totals alone, Consumer wins by a wide margin. But dividing sales by orders revealed a different story Home Office averages $243 per order vs Consumer's $225. The segment with the least volume has the highest value per transaction.

Where, what, and how they buy

Filtered to Home Office customers only and broke down by three dimensions: state (California leads with 325 orders), shipping preference (Standard Class dominates at 1,046 orders vs Same Day's 112), and most ordered product (Easy-staple Paper at 12 orders). Together these paint a clear operational picture of the segment.

05 — Key Findings
Geography

California shows up everywhere.

  • —Highest total sales at $446,306 485x more than North Dakota's $919
  • —Corporate segment in California alone ($144,731) exceeds North Dakota's full sales
  • —Top state for Home Office customers specifically, with 325 orders
  • —Business density, not just population, explains the gap
Segments & Products

The obvious metric isn't always the right one.

  • —Consumer has 5,101 orders vs Home Office's 1,746 but Home Office averages $243/order vs $225
  • —Staple Envelope: 47 orders, $1,675 total. Canon Copier: 5 orders, $61,599 total
  • —Home Office prefers Standard Class shipping (1,046 orders vs 112 Same Day)
  • —Most ordered Home Office product: Easy-staple Paper a recurring consumable, not a one-time purchase

07 — Conclusion

What the Store Should Do With This

Although the Consumer segment dominates in order volume and total revenue, the Home Office segment is worth closer attention its customers spend more per order on average. The store could benefit from focusing retention and marketing efforts on Home Office customers, particularly in California.

Standard Class shipping is the clear preference for Home Office ensuring it remains reliable and well-stocked is a low-effort way to protect the segment's satisfaction. And given that California consistently leads across every dimension of this analysis, any geographic campaign should start there.

08 — Reflection
Lesson 01

Define the question before touching the data

"Highest selling" sounds like one question. It's two. Catching that ambiguity before building the pivot table saved time and produced more honest findings.

Lesson 02

Totals hide what averages reveal

Consumer's total revenue looks dominant until you divide by order count. The Home Office insight only appears when you ask a different version of the same question.

Lesson 03

One finding should generate the next

California appearing in the state analysis prompted the Home Office drill-down. That drill-down prompted the shipping preference question. The best analysis is a chain, not a checklist.

Lesson 04

Simple tools are enough to find real insight

This entire analysis used pivot tables and basic arithmetic. The insight wasn't hidden in a complex model it was in asking the right question and looking at the answer from a different angle.
