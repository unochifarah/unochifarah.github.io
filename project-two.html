01 — Context

Querying Relational Data to Find Purchasing Patterns

The Chinook Database represents a complete digital media store ecosystem. It consists of 11 relational tables covering everything from purchase history to customer geography and media format preferences.

The goal of this project was to utilize PostgreSQL to traverse these complex data structures, surfacing actionable insights about store performance and customer behavior.

Analytical Questions

  • — Which artists, albums, and individual tracks sell the most?
  • — What is the single top-selling genre within each country?
  • — Which geographic regions order the most frequently?
  • — What are the maximum tracks bought in a single purchase?
  • — Which media type is overwhelmingly preferred by customers?
Database

11 Relational Tables

Structured data containing dedicated dimension tables for Artists, Albums, Tracks, Media Types, Genres, and Customers, revolving around Invoice and Invoice Line fact tables.

Approach

Step-by-Step Join Logic

Multi-table queries were written systematically starting from the core transactional fact table and using INNER JOINs to branch outward to necessary dimensional attributes.

02 — Approach

Map the Schema. Query the Insights.

Writing efficient SQL requires understanding the blueprint first. Before typing any commands, I broke down the relationships between tables to ensure my query paths were accurate and performant.

Step 01

Identify the Tables

For each question, I pinpointed exactly which dimension tables held the required descriptors (like genre name or artist name) and which fact tables held the transactional data.

Step 02

Break Down the Problem

Complex questions, like finding the top genre per country, were broken into smaller pieces. First, I needed to aggregate total sales by genre and country, before attempting to rank them.

Step 03

Build the Join Chain

I reliably started from the center the `invoice_line` table and joined outward. `invoice_line` connects to `track`, which connects to `album`, which finally connects to `artist`.

Step 04

Refine with Logic

Used `GROUP BY` and `ORDER BY DESC` to surface the highest metrics. For ranking subsets, I isolated the preliminary aggregations inside a CTE (Common Table Expression) to keep the final query clean.

03 — Process
01 / Navigating the Schema

Connecting the transactional to the descriptive

To calculate the best-selling artists, the database required a 3-table join. I pulled the quantity from `invoice_line`, joined it to the `track` table using `track_id`, bridged to the `album` table via `album_id`, and finally secured the artist's name by joining the `artist` table on `artist_id`.

INNER JOIN Primary Keys Foreign Keys
02 / Data Aggregation

Summarizing volume metrics

For questions regarding top performance (highest selling track, most popular media type), I utilized aggregate functions. Wrapping `il.quantity` in a `SUM()` function while grouping by the dimension name (`tr.name` or `mt.name`) allowed me to accurately stack-rank sales volume.

SUM() COUNT() GROUP BY
03 / Structuring with CTEs

Creating temporary result sets for clarity

To determine the top genre per country, writing one massive subquery would be unreadable. I structured the query using a `WITH` clause to create a CTE called `genre_sales`. This pre-calculated the total sales for every genre grouped by billing country, giving me a clean foundation for the next step.

WITH Clause CTE Query Architecture
04 / Advanced Window Functions

Isolating the top category per region

Referencing the CTE, I applied `RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY billing_country ORDER BY total DESC)`. This window function assigned a rank of 1 to the highest-selling genre inside each country partition. The final query simply selected rows where the rank equaled 1.

RANK() PARTITION BY Window Functions
04 — Deliverables

Complex Queries. Structural Clarity.

The core deliverable of this project is a series of precisely formatted, high-performance PostgreSQL queries. By maintaining a strict formatting standard, even multi-level nested window functions remain highly readable.

Concept 01

Multi-Table Joins

Used heavily across all queries, chaining up to 3 dimension tables to the core fact table to combine transactional data with descriptive names.

Concept 02

Grouping & Aggregations

Consistently applying `SUM()` for quantity totals and `COUNT()` for order volumes, coupled with structured `GROUP BY` clauses.

Concept 03

CTEs & Window Functions

Utilizing `WITH` clauses alongside `RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY)` to elegantly solve the classic 'top N per category' database problem.

05 — Key Findings
Content Strategy

Two distinct paths to artist success.

  • —Iron Maiden dominates overall as the top selling artist with 140 sales.
  • —Their track "The Trooper" is the #1 individual song (5 sales), showing broad catalog strength.
  • —Chico Buarque wins the album race with "Minha Historia" (27 sales) despite not hitting the top artist list.
  • —This shows a split behavior: some artists drive sales across their entire discography, while others rely on a single powerhouse record.
Market & Format

Rock music and MPEG audio rule globally.

  • —Rock dominates 92% of the market as the #1 genre in 23 out of 25 countries.
  • —Exceptions: Argentina ties Rock with Alternative & Punk; Sweden prefers Latin.
  • —MPEG audio is overwhelmingly dominant with 1,976 sales compared to just 146 for Protected AAC.
  • —Purchase sizes peak at exactly 14 tracks, a consistent limit hit by 59 different customers.

07 — Conclusion

What the Store Should Do With This

The store's strongest core market is the USA, with Rock music encoded in MPEG format being the absolute preference across both geographic and technical dimensions. The data suggests that classic rock back-catalogs (like Iron Maiden) drive high-volume repeat purchases across entire discographies.

To leverage this, the store should implement targeted promotions or bundle pricing focused heavily on Rock artists, specifically aimed at the USA and Canada. Given that purchase sizes organically cap at 14 tracks, offering 15-track bundles at a slight discount could effectively break the buying ceiling and increase average order value among existing users.

08 — Reflection
Lesson 01

Start joins at the fact table

Traversing a schema is much easier when you anchor your queries to the transactional center (`invoice_line`) and join outward to descriptive dimensions.

Lesson 02

CTEs prevent nested chaos

Trying to aggregate and rank data simultaneously leads to unreadable code. Using CTEs acts as a logical stepping stone, keeping complex multi-level queries clean.

Lesson 03

Different content behaves differently

Seeing Chico Buarque top the album charts but miss the artist charts proved that one-hit-wonder albums and catalog-wide artist dominance are two distinct business drivers.

Lesson 04

SQL formatting is a feature, not a detail

Strict adherence to indentation, capitalizing keywords, and using clear aliases makes troubleshooting massive analytical queries significantly faster.
