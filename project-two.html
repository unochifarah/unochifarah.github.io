Case Study 02

E-Commerce Customer Behavior Analysis

Tools PostgreSQL · Power BI
Dataset 500K+ Transactions · 8 Tables
Coverage 93K Customers · 27 States
Period 2016 – 2018
01 — Context

Revenue Was There. Customers Weren't Coming Back.

Olist, Brazil's largest e-commerce platform, had 93,000 customers and $15.4M in revenue — but underneath the numbers was a structural retention crisis. 80% of customers never placed a second order. No one had quantified how much revenue was silently walking out the door.

This project was built to surface that number, identify which customer segments were driving it, and provide a prioritized action plan for re-engagement — backed by RFM scoring and cohort retention analysis across the full transaction history.

Business questions answered:

  • — Which customers are most valuable, and how are they segmented by behavior?
  • — What percentage of customers have churned, and how much revenue is at risk?
  • — Which RFM segments should be prioritized for re-engagement campaigns?
  • — When do customers churn — and is it consistent across all cohorts?
  • — What is the estimated revenue recovery potential from targeted campaigns?
Key Finding

79.75% Revenue at Risk

$12.3M out of $15.4M total revenue is tied to churned customers. At Risk and Cannot Lose Them segments alone account for 53% of that — $6.5M needing immediate action.

Root Cause

Month-1 Retention Collapse

Cohort analysis across all monthly cohorts shows sub-1% month-1 retention — consistent across every signup month. The problem isn't seasonal. It's structural.

02 — Approach

How I Framed the Analysis

A 4-phase SQL pipeline — from raw schema design through RFM scoring to cohort churn analysis — culminating in a 3-page Power BI dashboard with quantified business recommendations.

Phase 01

Build the Foundation

Designed a normalized 8-table PostgreSQL schema with foreign keys and indexes. Loaded 500K+ rows from the Olist relational dataset — forcing real multi-table JOINs from day one.

Phase 02

Clean & Validate

Null checks, duplicate detection, date logic validation, and revenue sanity checks. Created a clean_orders view — 96K delivered orders with valid timestamps and positive payments — as the single source of truth for all downstream analysis.

Phase 03

Score & Segment

RFM scoring using NTILE(4) window functions across Recency, Frequency, and Monetary dimensions. Combined scores into 9 behavioral segments — Champions, Loyal, At Risk, Lost, and five others — with CASE WHEN segmentation logic.

Phase 04

Quantify the Risk

Churn flagging (90-day definition), cohort retention analysis by signup month, geographic breakdown by state, and revenue recovery estimates at 20% re-engagement — turning findings into an actionable campaign priority matrix.

03 — Process
01 / Schema Design & Data Loading

A real relational database, not a flat table

Designed a normalized PostgreSQL schema with 8 tables, proper foreign keys, and indexes before loading a single row. Load order was critical — customers and sellers before orders, orders before items and payments. Resolved FK violations and column naming mismatches during import.

PostgreSQL Schema Design Foreign Keys Indexing
02 / Data Exploration & Cleaning

Validating every assumption before analysis

Null checks across all key columns, duplicate order detection, date logic validation (delivered before purchased), and revenue sanity checks. Built the clean_orders view filtering to delivered orders with valid timestamps and positive payment values — 96,478 records forming the analysis foundation.

NULL Checks Date Validation Views FILTER WHERE
03 / RFM Segmentation

Window functions and CTEs as the centerpiece

Computed Recency (days since last order), Frequency (order count), and Monetary (total spend) per customer using a multi-CTE pipeline. Scored each dimension 1–4 using NTILE(4) window functions, then applied CASE WHEN segmentation logic to classify 93K customers into 9 behavioral groups. Saved as a materialized table for fast downstream querying.

CTEs NTILE() RANK() PARTITION BY CASE WHEN
04 / Churn Analysis & Recommendations

From observation to quantified action plan

Defined churn as no purchase in 90 days. Built a cohort retention table using DATE_TRUNC to track re-purchase rates by signup month — revealing sub-1% month-1 retention across all cohorts. Geographic breakdown identified SP as the dominant churn state at $4.83M. Revenue recovery estimates at 20% re-engagement produced a ranked campaign priority matrix.

05 — Key Findings
Churn & Revenue at Risk

80% of customers churned. 79.75% of revenue is at risk.

  • —$12.3M out of $15.4M total revenue tied to churned customers
  • —At Risk segment: highest churned revenue at $3.5M
  • —Champion segment: only 21% churn — the sole protected group
  • —SP state alone accounts for $4.83M churned — 3x second-highest RJ ($1.81M)
Cohort Retention

Sub-1% month-1 retention across every cohort — without exception.

  • —January 2017 cohort: 717 customers → 2 returned in month 1 (0.28%)
  • —February 2017 cohort: 1,628 customers → 3 returned in month 1 (0.18%)
  • —Pattern consistent across all cohorts — not seasonal, structural
  • —20% re-engagement on At Risk alone = estimated $708K recovered
  • —Potential Loyalist (23K customers, 60% churn) = highest conversion upside

07 — Reflection
Lesson 01

The retention problem was hiding in plain sight

$15.4M in revenue looked healthy until cohort analysis revealed that 80% of customers never came back. Top-line numbers are almost always misleading. The insight only appeared when I stopped aggregating and started tracking individual customer journeys over time.

Lesson 02

A clean view is worth more than raw tables

Creating the clean_orders view upfront — filtering once for delivered orders, valid timestamps, and positive payments — made every downstream query simpler and more trustworthy. Spending time on data quality before analysis is never wasted.

Lesson 03

Segments only matter if you act on them

Knowing a customer is "At Risk" is an observation. Knowing there are 17,579 of them, they last bought 278 days ago, they're worth $201 each, and a 20% recovery rate yields $708K — that's a campaign brief. The analysis was only complete once every finding had a dollar sign next to it.

Lesson 04

One intervention point explains everything

The cohort data showed the entire churn problem concentrated at month 0→1. That single finding collapsed a complex retention analysis into one concrete recommendation: a post-purchase email sequence within 7 days of first delivery. The simplest interventions are usually the ones the data points to.
