01 — Context

Understanding International Performance

This project analyzes international sales data derived from Microsoft's AdventureWorks 2014 sample database. The original SQL database was transformed into three CSV files covering sales orders, customer information, and vendor data.

The analysis focuses on exploring global sales performance and shipping behavior between 2011 and 2014, shifting from exploratory analysis in Excel to an interactive dashboard in Power BI.

Business Questions

  • — How is the company's overall sales performance?
  • — How has sales changed over time?
  • — Which countries contribute the most revenue?
  • — How do average order value and freight cost differ across countries?
  • — Why does Canada have the highest freight cost?
Dataset

AdventureWorks 2014

An established Microsoft SQL dataset transformed into 3 distinct CSV tables: Sales Order, Customer Master, and Vendor Master, tracking international transactions.

Approach

Validate First, Dashboard Later

Before touching Power BI, each business question was explored using Pivot Tables in Excel to validate key findings and build a stronger foundation for data modeling.

02 — Approach

From Raw Data to Interactive Logic.

The process moved linearly from extracting structured data out of a sample database to verifying hypothesis through simple pivots, before finally establishing relational models in Power BI.

Step 01

Map the Tables

For each question, I first identified the relevant tables and fields needed. Taking time to map Sales Orders to Customer and Vendor tables ensured accurate joins later.

Step 02

Excel Exploration

I explored the data using Pivot Tables in Excel to validate assumptions. By calculating averages and sums early, I knew exactly what metrics my dashboard needed to highlight.

Step 03

Data Modeling

Imported the validated CSVs into Power BI to build robust relationships between the tables. This step is critical for cross-filtering and ensuring metrics adapt correctly to slicers.

Step 04

Dashboard Design

Recreated the analysis visually by developing DAX-driven KPI measures and designing an interactive dashboard with clear slicers and drill-down functionality for international reporting.

03 — Process
01 / Global Performance & Time Trends

Tracking growth vs partial data drops

The analysis showed sales grew consistently from 2011 to 2013. A sudden dip in 2014 initially appeared as a performance issue, but context confirmed the dataset only contains data through June of that year, rather than reflecting an actual decline in performance.

Time Series Total Sales Data Context
02 / Revenue by Country

The United States leads the market

Comparing totals geographically revealed the United States is the company's strongest market, generating approximately $32 million in sales. In stark contrast, Germany recorded the lowest total sales at around $5 million, establishing clear upper and lower bounds for global operations.

Geography Revenue
03 / The Canada Anomaly

Fewer orders, but the highest freight cost

While the US had the highest order volume, Canada exhibited the highest average freight cost per order. This prompted a deeper look: if distance wasn't the main factor, why was shipping to Canada so expensive?

Freight Cost Order Volume Anomaly Detection
04 / Order Value Correlation

Order size drives shipping expenses

Digging into purchasing behavior solved the freight mystery: higher freight costs correlate strongly with larger order values, not geographic distance. Canada also had the highest average sales per order, which naturally required more expensive shipping.

05 — Key Findings
Growth & Markets

Steady growth, led by the US.

  • —Sales grew consistently across international territories from 2011 to 2013
  • —2014 appears lower simply because data stops in June
  • —The United States generated ~$32M, making it the strongest market by far
  • —Germany records the lowest total overall sales at around $5M
Freight & Orders

Volume drives shipping costs.

  • —Canada has the highest average freight cost despite fewer total orders than the US
  • —Higher freight expenses are directly driven by larger order values
  • —Geographic distance matters less than order density when determining shipping rates

07 — Conclusion

Connecting Purchasing Behavior to Costs

The United States remains the company's strongest market by total revenue, providing a stable baseline for global operations. However, Canada stands out for having the highest average order value alongside the highest freight cost.

The analysis suggests that freight expenses are primarily influenced by order size rather than physical location or distance. Combined with the consistent tax rate across all countries, this provides a clearer understanding of the company's international sales patterns and highlights exactly how customer purchasing behavior directly affects bottom-line shipping costs.

08 — Reflection
Lesson 01

Validate before visualizing

Jumping straight into Power BI can hide basic data truths. Exploring with Excel Pivot Tables first ensured the logic was sound before any dashboard building began.

Lesson 02

Contextualize drops in data

The sharp decline in 2014 sales looked like a massive business failure until checking the timestamps revealed the data simply stopped halfway through the year.

Lesson 03

Correlation isn't always geographic

It's easy to assume international freight costs are purely driven by borders and distance. Proving that it was actually tied to order value shifted the entire narrative.

Lesson 04

Averages reveal customer habits

While the US dominated total metrics, analyzing average sales per order showed that Canadian customers actually place larger individual orders on a per-transaction basis.
